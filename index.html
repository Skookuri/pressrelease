Schizophrenia

Unveiling the Language Puzzle

A Tufts-led team explores the mind's entangled web of language processing dysfunction in schizophrenic patients

by

Alyssa Williams

June 20, 2023
Tags: Schizophrenia , Psychology , Neuroscience , Mental Health , Graduate School of Arts and Sciences , School of Arts and Sciences

Schizophrenia casts a profound shadow over daily life- penetrating even the most intimate thoughts. Language, a fundamental tool for communication and understanding, is significantly affected by this complex disorder. Yet, a glimmer of hope emerges as groundbreaking research delves into the intricate interplay between language impairments and cognitive difficulties. These discoveries hold the key to unlocking more effective interventions and fostering improved outcomes for individuals grappling with schizophrenia.

At the Kuperberg NeuroCognition of Language Lab, nestled within the renowned Massachusetts General Hospital, Gina Kuperberg- the Dennett Stibel Professor of Cognitive Science- and her team embark on a transformative journey to unravel the mysteries of language in schizophrenia.

Traditionally, researchers studied four different linguistic components of schizophrenia in isolation, often overlooking their interconnected nature. These components include:

  1. Disorganized language output: This refers to incoherent speech patterns, commonly associated with thought disorder.
  2. Auditory Verbal Hallucinations (AVH): These experiences involve difficulties in distinguishing one's own inner speech from external speech.
  3. High-Level Semantic and Contextual Processing: Dysfunction in this area makes it challenging for individuals to comprehend complex sentences and understand the meaning conveyed within a given context.
  4. Low-Level Perception: This component describes sensory and perceptual deficits that affect the processing of acoustic and visual features fundamental to spoken and written language.

Recognizing the interconnectedness of these four linguistic components, the Kuperberg team adopted a generative framework, a conceptual model for understanding typical language processing. In doing so, they hypothesized, valuable insights could be gained by bringing the literature surrounding these components together.

This study's findings introduce an exciting perspective on language processing in schizophrenia. It suggests that our ability to comprehend and produce language relies on internal circuits that generate predictions about what we hear or say. These predictions are continuously updated based on new information we encounter. However, disruptions to these circuits in schizophrenia can compromise communication abilities, affecting both higher-level language understanding and lower-level speech perception. These disruptions may also contribute to thought-disordered speech and false perceptions, such as AVH. A combined approach targeting both high-level and low-level processes may have synergistic effects on cognitive and perceptual functioning, breaking the cycle of disrupted prediction and prediction error. Exploring the efficacy of a combined remediation approach in individuals at high risk for developing schizophrenia may help prevent or mitigate the onset of psychosis and language dysfunction.

Language abnormalities and social difficulties can be detected prior to illness onset, suggesting a developmental link. Early intervention targeting psychosocial, cognitive, and perceptual abilities could provide an optimal opportunity to regain control over abnormal prediction and belief updating in the course of schizophrenia.

This research is significant because it deepens our understanding of language abnormalities in schizophrenia. Exploring the interconnected nature of language impairments and cognitive difficulties opens doors for further investigation and the development of rehabilitation methods to enhance communication and thought processes in individuals living with schizophrenia.

Brown, M., & Kuperberg, G. R. (2015). A hierarchical generative framework of language processing: Linking language perception, interpretation, and production abnormalities in schizophrenia. Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 9. https://doi.org/10.3389/fnhum.2015.00643
